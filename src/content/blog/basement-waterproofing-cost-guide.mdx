## Average Basement Waterproofing Cost

According to HomeAdvisor and Angi regional data, basement waterproofing in the
Northeast typically costs between $3,000 and $10,000. The single biggest factor
is whether the work is done from the inside or the outside. Interior drainage
systems sit at the lower end, while full exterior excavation lands at the top,
and occasionally above it for large or hard-to-reach foundations.

For a rough mental model: a straightforward interior perimeter drain with a sump
pump in an average home is usually a mid-four-figure project. A failing wall that
needs excavation, a new membrane, and a footing drain is a five-figure one.

## What Affects the Price

Several variables explain why two homes on the same street get very different
quotes:

- **Method.** Interior versus exterior is the largest single driver, often a
  difference of several thousand dollars.
- **Linear footage.** Pricing usually scales with how much wall perimeter is
  treated, so larger basements cost more.
- **Severity of damage.** Active structural cracks or bowing walls add repair
  work on top of the waterproofing itself.
- **Water table and soil.** A high water table or dense clay soil increases the
  load on any system and can push you toward a more robust solution.
- **Add-ons.** Sump pumps, battery backups, and vapor barriers each add cost but
  are often worth it in a region prone to storm-driven power outages.
- **Site access.** Landscaping, decks, and driveways near the foundation raise
  the cost of exterior work.

## Interior vs Exterior: The Cost Difference

Interior waterproofing manages water after it enters. A contractor installs a
perimeter drain under the slab edge and routes water to a sump pump. Because no
excavation is involved, it is the less expensive route, generally $3,500 to
$6,500 in this region.

Exterior waterproofing stops water at the soil. It requires digging down to the
footing, sealing the wall with a membrane, and installing a footing drain. It is
the most durable fix and the right choice for actively failing walls, but the
excavation pushes it to $8,000 to $14,000 or more. For most existing homes with
seepage rather than structural failure, contractors recommend interior systems
first.

## How to Save Without Cutting Corners

The cheapest real fix is sometimes not waterproofing at all. Before committing to
a major system, have downspouts extended away from the house and check that soil
slopes away from the foundation. Poor grading and short downspouts cause a large
share of wet basements, and correcting them is inexpensive. If you do need a
system, scheduling outside of the spring rush can occasionally help, and bundling
the sump pump and backup into the original install is cheaper than adding them
later.

Avoid the temptation to hire the lowest bidder who skips the in-person
inspection. Underpricing usually means an incomplete diagnosis, and a system that
treats the wrong problem is the most expensive outcome of all.

## How to Get an Accurate Quote

Collect at least three written, itemized estimates. Insist that each contractor
inspect the basement in person, because foundation conditions vary too much for a
reliable phone quote. Ask each one to identify where the water is coming from,
not just what they would install. A contractor who explains the cause is far more
likely to fix it.

When you compare bids, line up the scope item by item. A low number that omits a
sump pump or a warranty is not actually lower.
