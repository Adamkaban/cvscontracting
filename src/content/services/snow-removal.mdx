In Maine and New Hampshire, snow removal is not a luxury, it is how you keep a
property usable and safe from November through April. This guide covers how snow
removal services work, what they cost, and how to decide between paying per storm
and locking in a seasonal contract.

## How Snow Removal Services Work

Most contractors start with a site walk to map your driveway or lot, note where
snow can be piled, and flag obstacles like culverts, garden beds, and pavement
edges. They set a trigger depth, usually 2 to 3 inches, at which service begins
automatically so you are not calling during every storm. Clearing is done with a
plow, a blower for tight areas, and de-icing with salt or sand for traction.
During a long storm, a contractor may return several times to keep up.

## Per-Visit vs Seasonal Contracts

This is the decision that matters most for your budget.

### Per-Visit

You pay each time the contractor clears the property. According to HomeAdvisor and
Angi regional figures, a residential driveway runs roughly $30 to $80 per visit.
Per-visit works well for short, flat driveways and lighter winters, but in a big
snow year the visits add up fast and you may wait longer for service.

### Seasonal Contract

You pay a flat rate for the whole winter, commonly $300 to $600 for a typical
residential driveway, with larger and commercial properties higher. The appeal is
predictable cost and priority on the route during heavy storms. In a region that
regularly sees a foot of snow in a single event, most homeowners find the
certainty worth it.

## What Drives the Price

Pricing depends on the size of the driveway or lot, the surface type, how steep
or long the drive is, the typical snowfall in your area, and whether salting or
sanding is included. Commercial properties cost more because of larger areas and
the liability of keeping entrances and walkways safe. Confirm whether de-icing is
bundled or billed separately, since that is the most common surprise on a winter
invoice.

## Book Before the First Storm

The best contractors fill their routes before winter starts. Booking in early
fall locks in your price and guarantees a spot, while waiting until the first
storm often means a long wait or no availability. If you manage a commercial
property, line up a contract well ahead, because safe access is a liability issue
the moment the lot opens.

## How to Hire the Right Contractor

Get at least three written quotes that state the trigger depth, what is included,
and whether salting is extra. Confirm the contractor is insured, which matters
most on commercial lots, and ask about response time during major storms. Recent
local references tell you whether the contractor actually shows up when the snow
is heavy.
