Excavation is the groundwork that every other part of a project depends on. Done
right, foundations sit level, water drains away from the house, and utilities run
clean. Done poorly, you inherit settling, drainage problems, and expensive
corrections later. This guide covers what excavation contractors do, what it
costs in Maine and New Hampshire, and when you need a pro.

## What Excavation Contractors Actually Do

Excavation is a broad trade. A single contractor may handle site prep for a new
build one week and a drainage trench the next. Common jobs include clearing and
stripping a lot, cutting and filling to a target grade, digging foundation and
footing trenches, trenching for water, sewer, and electrical service, installing
septic systems, and rough grading so the finished site sheds water correctly.

## Why the Region Matters

Maine and New Hampshire soil is not forgiving. Ledge, the shelf of bedrock that
sits close to the surface in much of the region, can turn a routine dig into a
job that needs hammers or blasting. Rocky, glacial soil slows machinery and adds
haul-away volume. Frost depth also drives how deep footings and utility lines
must sit, which affects how much a contractor has to excavate. A local crew
prices these conditions because they deal with them every day.

## What Drives the Price

The cost of an excavation job comes down to a handful of factors:

- **Scope.** Grading a small area is a different project from full foundation
  site prep.
- **Soil and rock.** Ledge and boulders are the most common reasons a quote
  comes in high.
- **Volume.** More material moved, and more trips to haul it away, means more
  cost.
- **Access.** Tight lots, long driveways, and distance to a dump site all add
  time.
- **Permits and utility marking.** Required on most structural and utility work.

According to HomeAdvisor and Angi regional figures, excavation projects in the
Northeast commonly run $1,500 to $12,000, with small grading at the low end and
new-home site prep at the top.

## Before the Crew Arrives

Call 811 before any digging so underground utilities are located and marked. It
is free and legally required, and it prevents strikes on gas, electric, water,
and communication lines. If your project touches a foundation, septic system, or
changes site grading, confirm permit requirements with the local code office.
Foundation work often pairs with [foundation repair](/foundation-repair) or
drainage, so it is worth planning those together.

## How to Hire the Right Contractor

Get at least three written, itemized estimates that spell out scope, soil
assumptions, and haul-away. Confirm the contractor is licensed and insured, and
ask how they handle ledge if it is found, since that is the most common source of
change orders. Recent local references matter, because excavation quality is hard
to judge once the site is backfilled.
