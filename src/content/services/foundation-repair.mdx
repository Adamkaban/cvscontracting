A foundation problem rarely stays a foundation problem. Left alone, a settling
corner becomes sticking doors, then cracked drywall, then a wall that no longer
carries load the way it should. This guide explains what foundation repair
involves, what it costs across Maine and New Hampshire, and how to tell a
cosmetic crack from a structural warning sign.

## Why Foundations Fail in Maine and New Hampshire

The two forces behind most foundation damage in this region are water and frost.
Soil that holds water expands and contracts, and that movement pushes against
foundation walls. Frost heave makes it worse: when wet soil freezes it lifts,
and when it thaws it drops, cycling on the foundation every winter. Add poor
grading or clogged gutters that dump water at the base of the house, and even a
sound foundation starts to move.

Older homes are not the only ones at risk. New construction on backfilled or
poorly compacted soil can settle within a few years.

## Common Repair Methods

The right fix depends entirely on what is wrong, which is why an in-person
inspection matters before any quote.

### Crack Repair

Thin, non-structural cracks are sealed with epoxy or polyurethane injection that
fills the crack and keeps water out. This is the least expensive repair,
generally $500 to $1,500 per HomeAdvisor and Angi regional figures.

### Piering and Underpinning

When a foundation has settled, contractors install piers, steel pushed down to
stable soil or bedrock, to support and often lift the foundation back toward
level. Pricing scales with the number of piers, and a full underpinning job can
reach $10,000 to $15,000 or more.

### Wall Stabilization

Bowing or leaning walls, common where soil pressure is high, are stabilized with
carbon-fiber straps, steel I-beams, or wall anchors. Cost depends on the wall
length and how far it has moved.

## Signs You Should Not Ignore

Some cracks are cosmetic and some are structural. The ones to act on are
stair-step cracks in block or brick, horizontal cracks, walls bowing inward,
sloping floors, and doors or windows that suddenly stick. Gaps opening where
walls meet the ceiling or floor point to active movement. If you see these, get
an inspection before the next freeze-thaw season makes the repair larger.

## What Drives the Price

The biggest cost factors are the type of damage, the repair method, the number
of piers or anchors required, and how accessible the foundation is. Excavation
to reach an exterior footing adds cost, as does any drainage work needed to keep
the problem from returning. Many foundation repairs in the region are paired with
[basement waterproofing](/basement-waterproofing) because water is usually part
of the cause.

## How to Hire the Right Contractor

Get at least three written, itemized estimates and make sure each contractor
inspects in person. Ask what is causing the movement, not just what they would
install, and confirm the warranty is transferable. Be wary of any company that
recommends extensive piering without explaining the underlying cause.
