A foundation problem rarely stays contained. Left alone, a settling corner produces sticking doors, then cracked drywall, then walls that no longer carry load correctly. In Maine and New Hampshire, frost heave and saturated spring soil can turn a small crack into a structural failure over just a few seasons. This guide covers what repairs cost here, which methods apply to which problems, and what to confirm before you hire a contractor.

## What Causes Foundation Problems in Maine and New Hampshire

Most foundation damage in this region traces back to water and frost, often acting together. Soil that holds water expands and contracts with temperature, and that pressure against foundation walls accumulates over years. Frost heave is the specific version of this problem: wet soil freezes, lifts, then thaws and drops. Each cycle stresses footings and walls. Add poor site grading or gutters that discharge at the base of the house, and even a sound foundation begins to shift.

Maine and New Hampshire also have significant older housing stock. Homes built before 1960 often have stone or old brick foundations where mortar erodes over decades. Block foundations from the 1960s through 1980s are prone to horizontal cracking under sustained soil pressure. Neither type was typically built with today's drainage standards or frost-depth compliance requirements.

New construction is not immune. Homes built on poorly compacted backfill can begin settling within a few years of completion, particularly in areas with soft or fill soil.

## Foundation Repair Methods and Costs

The right repair depends entirely on what is wrong. An in-person inspection, not a quote by phone or photo, is the required starting point for any legitimate job.

## Stone and Block Foundations: What Regional Homeowners Should Know

Many homes in Maine and New Hampshire have stone, brick, or concrete block foundations that require different approaches than poured concrete. Mortar repointing and parging can stabilize mild deterioration in stone and brick. Active displacement, significant bowing, or collapse requires more involved structural work, sometimes including partial or full replacement.

Not all contractors work on historic stone or brick foundations. Ask specifically about experience with these materials before proceeding. A contractor who specializes in poured concrete pier systems may not be the right person for an 1890 fieldstone basement.

New Hampshire does not require a statewide general contractor license. Contractors must register with the state, and most municipalities require permits for structural foundation work. Your contractor should obtain the permit, and you should verify before work begins.

## Repair or Replace: How to Make the Call

Repair is appropriate when the structural elements are still sound, the cause of movement can be addressed, and pier or stabilization systems can stop the progression. Many homeowners in [Lewiston, ME](/foundation-repair/lewiston-me) and [Nashua, NH](/foundation-repair/nashua-nh) repair and stabilize foundations that continue to function well for the life of the home.

Replacement makes more sense when the foundation is too deteriorated to repair reliably, when repair costs approach full replacement costs, or when a structural engineer recommends it. For older stone foundations, replacement is sometimes the only way to achieve current frost-depth compliance and drainage standards.

Get an independent structural engineering opinion before any major decision. Some foundation contractors are motivated to recommend the most expensive solution. An engineer's report costs $300 to $600 and can save thousands.

## Red Flags When Hiring a Foundation Contractor

- Recommends piering without explaining what is causing the movement
- Will not provide references from recent local jobs in Maine or New Hampshire
- Warranty does not transfer to future owners
- Refuses to discuss repair vs replacement options
- Provides a quote over the phone without an in-person inspection
- Cannot explain how drainage will be addressed to prevent recurrence
