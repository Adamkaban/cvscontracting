Basement waterproofing protects the lowest level of your home from water that
moves through soil, foundation walls, and the slab. In Maine and New Hampshire,
where ground stays saturated through spring snowmelt and freeze-thaw cycles open
new cracks each winter, a dry basement is rarely something you get to take for
granted. This guide explains how waterproofing works, what it costs across the
region, and how to hire a contractor who will fix the cause rather than paint
over the symptom.

## Why Wet Basements Are Common in Maine and New Hampshire

Two regional factors drive most basement water problems here. The first is
hydrostatic pressure. When snow melts or heavy rain falls, the soil around your
foundation holds water, and that water pushes against walls and the slab. Older
poured and block foundations were not always built with reliable drainage, so
the water finds the path of least resistance through cracks, cove joints, and
porous concrete.

The second is freeze-thaw movement. Water that sits in a small crack expands
when it freezes, widening the crack a little more each season. Over several
winters a hairline crack can become an active leak. This is why a problem that
looks minor in October can be a steady trickle by April.

## Interior vs Exterior Waterproofing

There are two broad approaches, and the right one depends on how water is
getting in and how much disruption you can accept.

### Interior Waterproofing

Interior systems manage water after it enters rather than stopping it at the
soil. The most common solution is an interior drain tile system: a contractor
cuts a channel around the perimeter of the basement floor, installs perforated
pipe, and routes water to a sump pit where a pump discharges it outside. Many
crews pair this with a vapor barrier on the walls.

Interior work is less invasive because it does not require digging up the yard.
It is also the more affordable route. According to HomeAdvisor and Angi regional
data, interior drainage systems generally run $4,000 to $7,000. For most existing
homes in the region, this is the method contractors recommend first.

### Exterior Waterproofing

Exterior waterproofing stops water at the source. The contractor excavates down
to the footing, cleans the foundation wall, applies a waterproof membrane, and
installs a footing drain that carries water away. It is the most thorough fix,
and it is the right call when walls are actively failing or when grading sends
water straight at the foundation.

It is also the most expensive and disruptive option, since it involves
excavation. Exterior projects typically range from $8,000 to $15,000 for larger
foundations, again per HomeAdvisor and Angi figures. Landscaping, driveways, and
decks near the wall can raise the price further.

## Drainage, Sump Pumps, and Grading

Waterproofing rarely works in isolation. A sump pump is the heart of most
interior systems, and a battery backup is worth the added cost in a region where
the heaviest water often arrives during the same storms that knock out power.
Outside the house, gutters that discharge well away from the foundation and soil
graded to slope away from the walls do a surprising amount of the work. A good
contractor will look at these before quoting a major system, because sometimes
the cheapest real fix is regrading and extending downspouts.

## Signs You Should Not Wait On

Some symptoms point to cosmetic moisture, and others point to structural risk.
White, chalky deposits on the wall, called efflorescence, mean water is moving
through the concrete. A musty smell signals trapped moisture and possible mold.
Standing water after a storm is a clear drainage failure. The symptom to treat
as urgent is a horizontal crack in a foundation wall, or a wall that is bowing
inward, since both can indicate the wall is losing the fight against soil
pressure. If you see either, have a contractor inspect it promptly.

## What Drives the Price

Beyond the interior-versus-exterior choice, the biggest cost factors are the
linear footage of wall being treated, the severity of existing damage, soil and
water-table conditions on your lot, and whether a sump pump or full drain
system is added. Site access matters too. A walkout basement is easier and
cheaper to work on than one surrounded by mature landscaping or hardscape.

## How to Hire the Right Contractor

Get at least three written, itemized estimates so you are comparing the same
scope. Confirm the contractor carries current liability insurance, ask for
recent local references, and read the warranty closely, since transferable
warranties add real value at resale. Be cautious of any company that quotes a
large exterior excavation without first ruling out simpler drainage and grading
fixes.
